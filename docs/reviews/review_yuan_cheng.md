# Review by Yuan Cheng (probabilistic_modeling_researcher)

## Project: SemRec: CEGAR Verification Oracle for LLM C-to-Rust Translation

**Reviewer Expertise:** Probabilistic modeling, statistical methodology, Bayesian inference, uncertainty quantification, experimental design, variance analysis, confidence intervals, sample size planning.

**Overall Score:** weak accept

---

## Summary
SemRec is a Z3-backed semantic equivalence verifier for C-to-Rust function translation that leverages a σ-bridge to capture cross-language semantic gaps invisible at the LLVM IR level. The system achieves 84.2% accuracy on 202 benchmark pairs and a 42.5% CEGAR convergence rate on 40 functions. While the deterministic verification core is principled, the experimental evaluation requires stronger statistical rigor—particularly around the stochastic CEGAR loop and the representativeness of the benchmark suite.

## Strengths
- **Substantial benchmark scale with measurable effect sizes.** The expansion from the earlier 32-pair benchmark to 202 pairs across 11 categories significantly improves statistical power. At n=202, the 84.2% accuracy (170/202) yields a 95% Wilson confidence interval of approximately [78.6%, 88.7%], which is narrow enough to be informative. The 70% definitive-verdict rate (142/202) similarly has a useful CI of roughly [63.3%, 76.0%].
- **Clean separation of deterministic and stochastic components.** The soundness theorem (UNSAT → equivalence on well-defined inputs) is entirely deterministic and does not rely on probabilistic arguments. This is the correct design: the formal core provides hard guarantees, and only the CEGAR repair loop introduces stochasticity via the LLM. This separation makes the system's reliability profile much easier to reason about.
- **The σ-bridge ablation demonstrates a strong causal effect.** Removing the σ-bridge drops accuracy from 80% to 40% on the ablation subset—a 40 percentage-point drop. Even on a 10-pair ablation set, a McNemar test on matched pairs would yield p < 0.05 for this magnitude of effect, suggesting the σ-bridge is genuinely load-bearing and not a small-sample artifact.
- **Sub-second verification enables large-scale statistical studies.** The average verification time under 1 second per pair means that future work can easily scale to thousands of pairs with bootstrap resampling, permutation tests, and Monte Carlo analyses of CEGAR convergence without prohibitive computational cost.
- **Transparent grounding of claims.** The grounding.json explicitly maps each claim (202 pairs, 84.2% accuracy, 42.5% CEGAR convergence) to specific artifacts and marks them as verified. This level of evidential traceability is uncommon and appreciated.

## Weaknesses
- **CEGAR convergence lacks distributional analysis.** The 42.5% convergence rate (17/40) is reported as a point estimate without confidence intervals, variance decomposition, or distributional characterization. With n=40, the 95% Wilson CI is approximately [28.0%, 58.2%]—a very wide interval. The breakdown into 70% for non-UB functions (14/20) has a CI of roughly [47.5%, 85.5%]. These intervals should be reported. Furthermore, the number of iterations to convergence is not characterized: is it geometric, heavy-tailed, or bimodal? This matters for deployment time budgeting.
- **Ablation sample size is critically small.** The ablation study uses only 10 pairs. At this sample size, the comparison between Full (80%) and Random Testing (80%) is essentially uninformative—a two-proportion z-test has zero power to detect anything less than a 30+ percentage-point difference. The ablation needs to be scaled to at least 50–100 pairs to draw reliable conclusions about the relative contribution of each pipeline stage.
- **No analysis of LLM temperature or prompt sensitivity.** The CEGAR loop uses GPT-4.1-nano with max 5 iterations, but no analysis is provided of how sensitive convergence rates are to LLM hyperparameters (temperature, top-p, prompt phrasing). A single run at one temperature provides a point estimate of a stochastic process; proper experimental design would involve multiple independent runs at each setting to estimate variance components.
- **Benchmark representativeness is uncharacterized.** The 202 pairs are described as "synthetic+real-pattern," but no sampling methodology or representativeness argument is offered. Without understanding the population these 202 pairs are drawn from, generalization is limited. Are these pairs i.i.d. samples from some distribution over C/Rust function pairs? Were they adversarially selected? The absence of this discussion limits the external validity of all reported metrics.
- **Missing multiple-comparison corrections.** The paper reports results across multiple subsets (11 categories, UB vs non-UB, ablation conditions) without any correction for multiple testing. With 11 categories, at least a Bonferroni or Holm correction should be applied when comparing per-category accuracy rates to avoid inflating the apparent significance of category-level findings.

## Detailed Comments
From a statistical methodology perspective, SemRec occupies an interesting position: its deterministic core provides exact guarantees (soundness modulo frontend correctness), but its evaluation and the CEGAR loop introduce stochastic elements that demand proper uncertainty quantification. The expansion to 202 benchmark pairs is a significant improvement over earlier versions, and the grounding.json provides welcome transparency. However, the evaluation still treats the CEGAR loop as if it were deterministic—reporting single point estimates without variance or distributional analysis.

I recommend: (1) reporting Wilson confidence intervals for all proportion-based metrics, (2) running the CEGAR experiment with at least 5 independent seeds per function to estimate iteration-count variance and convergence reliability, (3) scaling the ablation to 50+ pairs, and (4) providing a power analysis showing what effect sizes the current sample sizes can detect. The UB-wall finding (0/11 UB functions repaired) is statistically robust even at small n—it is genuinely informative that the failure rate is 100% for this subclass—but the positive convergence claims require larger samples. With these additions, the statistical foundation would match the quality of the formal verification core, and I would move to a clear accept.
